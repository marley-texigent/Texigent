Next-to-Skin
The fabric closest to the body
is the one that has to respond to it.

Skin contact is not a passive condition. The body's thermal output, moisture production, and sensitivity to texture change across every activity and environment — and the fabric against skin has to move with all of it. These materials are selected for how they perform across the full dynamic range of the body: thermoregulation, moisture transport, and next-to-skin comfort treated as specification variables, not afterthoughts.

Moisture Management
Wet skin is not a comfort problem. It is a performance problem.

Moisture retained at the skin surface increases friction, accelerates chilling during stop-and-go activities, and prolongs recovery between efforts. The failure is not sweat — it is a fabric that holds it there.

Fabric

Specified for high-output activities with variable intensity — trail running, cycling, alpine approach — where sustained moisture at skin is the primary comfort failure.

Insulation
Insulation fails at the point
of maximum effort. That is exactly
when it needs to work.

Breathability is the underspecified variable in insulation. Warmth ratings describe static conditions — the body in motion generates a different set of demands entirely. These materials are selected against activity and environment: how the fabric manages heat and vapour under output, not just how it performs at rest.

Problems in this category
1
Overheating
The insulation layer that keeps you warm at rest becomes the problem the moment you move.

Conventional insulation traps heat without discrimination — it performs identically whether the body is generating 200 watts or 20. Under sustained output, that retention becomes a liability.

A
Fabric
Warm when you stop. Not when you don't need it to be.
Active Insulation Fleece · Open-net construction with loft · TBC g/m²
An open-net foundation with loft plated onto it separates the two functions conventional insulation conflates. Under output, the open construction allows heat and vapour to pass through efficiently. At rest or under a shell, the loft engages and thermal retention holds. More breathable than bonded insulation; more thermally retentive than open-mesh fleece.

Shell
Protection is the easy part.
Managing output is the problem.

Every shell decision is a negotiation between what it keeps out and what it lets through. The fabrics here are selected for brands who understand that breathability is not a marketing term — it is the primary performance variable in sustained-output garments.

Problems in this category
1
Airflow vs. Structure
A tightly woven shell stops the wind. It also stops the body from breathing.

Weave density is a direct trade-off between protection and ventilation. Close the structure and airflow collapses. Open it and durability suffers. The specification decision is not which problem to accept — it is how to engineer around both.

A
Fabric
Airflow is a specification. So is the structure that survives it.

Engineered construction controls airflow without sacrificing structural integrity. High-tenacity yarn maintains tear strength and abrasion resistance at the thread level — so the weave can be opened for ventilation without compromising longevity.

Outer
Outer is not a protection category.
It is a construction category.

Without a membrane, there is no fallback. The fabric's construction, yarn behaviour, and surface character carry the entire performance brief — abrasion resistance, weight efficiency, stretch, drape, and tactile quality are all resolved at the material level.

End-of-Life Design
Built to last.
Built to end.
Both are specifications.

Most material decisions optimise for one end of the product lifecycle. These address both. Selected for mono-material integrity, certified recycled content, and lower-impact fibre inputs — the specification work is done at the front end so the end-of-life path is already built in.
